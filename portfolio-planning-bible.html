What is portfolio planning?

Portfolio planning is a strategic process that aligns product development with business objectives, market needs, and customer expectations. It involves analyzing market trends, assessing internal capabilities, and prioritizing initiatives to create a balanced and profitable product portfolio.

Steps in Portfolio Planning

1

Market Analysis

Begin by thoroughly analyzing the market landscape. Understand market trends, customer preferences, and competitor strategies. Identify emerging opportunities and potential threats. This analysis provides valuable insights into where your products stand in the market and what adjustments may be needed to strengthen your portfolio.

2

Assessment of Current Portfolio

Evaluate each product in your portfolio individually. Consider factors like revenue generation, profitability, market share, and strategic alignment with your business goals. Conduct a SWOT analysis for each product. This assessment helps you understand which improvement or phase-out.

3

Alignment with Business Objectives

Ensure that your product portfolio aligns with the overall business objectives, vision, and mission. Identify specific objectives you aim to achieve through your product offerings, such as market expansion, revenue growth, or customer acquisition.

4

Segmentation and Prioritization

Segment your target market based on relevant criteria such as demographics, psychographics, and buying behavior. Prioritize products and market segments based on their attractiveness and strategic importance. Focus your resources on high-potential products and market segments while deprioritizing or discontinuing products that no longer align with your target audience or business objectives.

5

Resource Allocation

Allocate resources strategically to support your portfolio strategy. It includes financial, human, and technological resources. Invest in product development, marketing, and sales initiatives that align with your prioritized products and market segments. Consider reallocating resources from low-performing products to those with higher growth potential.

6

Risk Management

Identify and mitigate risks associated with your product portfolio. This includes market risks, such as changing consumer preferences or competitive dynamics, as well as internal risks like product quality issues or resource constraints. Regularly monitor market conditions and performance metrics to stay proactive in risk management.

Portfolio Planning Methodologies

BCG Matrix

A strategic tool that categorizes products based on market growth rate and relative market share:

  • Stars: High growth, high market share
  • Cash Cows: Low growth, high market share
  • Question Marks: High growth, low market share
  • Dogs: Low growth, low market share
learn more

Ansoff Matrix

A framework for growth opportunities based on market and product dimensions:

  • Market Penetration: Existing products in existing markets
  • Market Development: Existing products in new markets
  • Product Development: New products in existing markets
  • Diversification: New products in new markets
learn more

Eisenhower Matrix

A prioritization tool based on urgency and importance:

  • Do First: Urgent and important
  • Schedule: Not urgent but important
  • Delegate: Urgent but not important
  • Eliminate: Neither urgent nor important
learn more

Kano Model

A methodology for categorizing customer requirements:

  • Must-be Quality: Basic requirements
  • One-dimensional Quality: Performance-related features
  • Attractive Quality: Unexpected delights
  • Indifferent Quality: Features with no impact

Which Specific Information Should the Portfolio Strategy Contain?

Objectives

  • defining the overall objectives and vision
  • outlining each product's role in achieving the goals

Analysis

  • market analysis data
  • markets trends
  • customer preferences
  • competitor activities

Resource allocation

  • financial resource
  • human resource
  • technological resource
  • marketing resource
